Creating a global network of females in STEM to make them count

The Problem

According to a 2021 Pew research conducted in the United States,

Women now earn a majority of all undergraduate and advanced degrees, but their share is less in STEM fields.

In STEM, women make up a large majority of all workers in health-related jobs, but are highly underrepresented in fields like engineering and computer science.

Black and Hispanic workers remain underrepresented in the STEM workforce (9 percent and 8 percent, respectively) compared with their share of all workers (11 percent and 17 percent, respectively).


Also, recent statistics reported by the United Nations shows that women make up 8% of the global total in manufacturing, construction and engineering programs and only 5% of all mathematics and statistics students, globally, are women.

The Need for Change

If females make up nearly half of the global population, then it makes up nearly half of the world’s intellect and potential.
Throughout the history of STEM. women have broken through “impossible” barricades, and are still doing so.
From the likes of Elizebeth Friedman and Katherine Johnson to the recent likes of Katherine Freese and Pardis Sabeti, the intellectual contribution of females in STEM must be embraced.

The Way Forward

The Female Greatness is aimed at minimizing the gender disparity in STEM by increasing the population and inclusion of females in STEM through a global network.
